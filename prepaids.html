Transaction facts

Disclosing fees on prepaid cards

Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) ·

Making the fine print behind prepaid cards easier to understand [no pun intended].

Read a 0-minute case study

Prepaid cards are sort of like debit or credit cards...but not really. To understand their impact on consumers, it's best to think of them literally–a card you purchase. Whichever way you think of it, prepaid cards have become popular, and they make industry a lot of money with undisclosed and intentionally confusing fees.

The CFPB is working on an upcoming rule to protect consumers from these practices. As a user experience designer, I helped prototype a label that encourages consumers to comparison shop and stay away from shady deals.

Context

Currently, each prepaid card company's retail package discloses different information. Each has a different style and format. We think these things make it difficult to find the information important to consumers, and do side-by-side comparisons. When most consumers need to limit their shopping time to the checkout line, it's no surprise that 68% of them don't comparison shop before making a purchase.

Taking regulatory and design inspiration from the Schumer box, which summarizes the costs of a credit card in the United States, and the Nutrition Facts label, which is required on most packaged foods, the CFPB wants to clearly present a prepaid card's most important fees so that consumers can find the best one for their needs.

Identifying design problems

Initially, the Design Ops team was asked to develop a way to visually compare fees in the hopes that they'd motivate consumers to comparison shop and read disclosures fully before making a purchase. The business had contracted a testing firm who would test two versions of design work. Based on the impressions of consumer testers, the preferred version would be chosen as a model form for the prepaids industry.

The problem was that the Design Ops team had just gotten this UX designer and they needed to put her on something, and they made this project her first victim. She asked the business some hard questions like, "How do you assume your work will actually do what you want it to do?" and "Why did you choose the content on the form?" They didn't get think this was important, and they got kinda grumpy, but that was okay.
