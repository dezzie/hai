HUM•DAH

The Home Mortgage Disclosure Act (HMDA)

Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) ·

Modernizing government data into useful tools for both experts and the curious.

Read a 6-minute case study

Each year, the CFPB publishes data about mortgages to the public as part of the Home Mortgage Disclosure Act ("Hum-Dah" for short). HMDA data are a view of the mortgage market, an important part of the American economy. But with millions of records reported by thousands of financial institutions each year, working with the data can be overwhelming. As a user experience designer (and occasional code contributor), I helped deliver a tool that enables people to get as much or as little data as they want.

Discovery

1.

With two product owners representing different industries, participatory design allowed for better collaboration during the discovery phase of the project. The first needfinding session revealed that different audience prioritizations favored one product owner over another. To move forward we detached UX success away from a traditional persona deliverable and focused on what best drove a conversation between the stakehodlers.

We diagrammed all potential audiences across two themes: technical needs and subject matter expertise.


Bringing a visual representation of different workflows to the table (no pun intended) facilitated relative, not ad-hoc, prioritization. This paradigm led the product owners to prioritize building a tool for audiences with high subject matter expertise, but varying degrees of technical needs.

With creative problem solving, I focused on the goals of UX over of its deliverables. I convincingly pitched a project vision to two product owners in a way that made them both feel heard and unified everyone on the team.

Concept

2.

People who already know a lot about HMDA also knew where to go for answers. The challenge of designing a tool to deliver value over existing sources was about improved workflow. We learned from focus groups, interviews, and a competitor analysis that the vast majority users found it difficult and annoying to download full annual data (about 12GB per year) and use a Windows-only tool to sift through records.

Making sense of it the data is particularly challenging for non-experts. Raw data uses codes instead of plain language to describe the information within, and


finding simple trends is difficult without powerful statistics software. Some ready-made reports are made available, but we heard from people that they would prefer to answer their own questions and tell their own stories.

Based on what we heard from users, I sketched a user flow for experts and non-experts in order of basic to complex tasks; a broad to granular data experience. This sketch identified features that would comprise the tool: a way to sift through data easily and accurately, and a way to summarize up to millions of records in ways that are easier to think about.

Forming Interactions

The HMDA UI guides people through narrowing down large parts of data into information they care about. In many cases, this will still be millions of records–and it can be overwhelming even for the most expert users. To make a more user-friendly experience, I was influenced by research on how people wayfind, focus attention, keep track of information, get overloaded by information, and make sense of abstract terms using plain language. Beware, I'm going to talk nerdy in this section.

3.

HMDA data is published in nondelimited text files. Think of the green streams of text from The Matrix. We are not quite there yet, so it adds steps to expert and non-expert workflows.

Data is also not categorized in any way. To free up short-term memory, we created categories of data and organized filters within by usefulness.


We used the ±7 principle to choose the best form fields for each filter. Some variables have hundreds of possible values, making searcahble drop-down fields especially helpful.

The result is a plain language tool that makes narrowing down millions of records much easier.

Non-experts can get context and assistance without having to consult outside resources. Visual cues built into filters also indicate whether a type of information can have multiple values.

Possibly the coolest thing though, are natural language features which summarize filtered data into one tidy sentence, or warn against downloading files too large for common software. It gets hard to keep track of so much information, regardless of expertise.

The tool solves the problem of finding trends and patterns in the data easily. For more granular insight, filtered data can be summarized, grouped, or otherwise chunked in ways that are meaningful to people.

This was a long-winded way of saying that this tool is user-friendly, intuitive, and fast.

Iteration

We performed usability testing at various stages of development with expert and non-expert audiences to figure out our pain points and to plan future iterations. Because we also had specific goals for UI elements, particularly around using plain language while preserving familiarity, our usability testing went beyond "general impression"-types of tasks. Github played a major role in relaying feedback, using Issues to request, discuss, and track changes. It allowed the entire team to join the conversation and follow UX's process–especially when we had to do it again the next year. With a new team. In two months.
