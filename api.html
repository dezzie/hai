Home Mortgage Disclosure Act (Hum-Dah)

Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) ·

Engaging the developer community with better, more usable software documentation.
Lean UX · Front-end development

Read a 0-minute case study

The CFPB believes that making public data easy to use is necessary for government transparency. It's a major part of why they release open source software and engage the developer community. With the launch of HMDA, the Bureau saw the perfect opportunity to showcase its toughest API, but they wanted to get their documentation right. Filling both UX and front-end roles, I worked with a lean UX mindset to design content with quick wins and hands-on examples. And in collaboration with 18f, I engaged the local developer community for usability testing and feedback. It's an open effort to maintain content that solves problems–and it's why it has set the standard for software documentation in the federal government.

Concept

The discovery phase of the HMDA project suggested that in order to reach the developer community, we would need to create a separate engagement effort. With API documentation that addressed developers first and not as adjuncts to advocacy groups or mortgage data experts, devs would better see the CFPB as a potential resource or inspiration for their own work.

A heuristic analysis of current project and CFPB documentation saw an opportunity for a more unified brand identity and voice. Other government documentation websites showed a need for living, usable, findable information, with good context and background, and a clear value proposition.

Working closely with the API's author, Clinton Dreisbach, and visual designer Mehgan Iulo, our concept was designed to meet these goals:

  1. Bring content in line with CFPB brand and voice
  2. Edit content to speak to HMDA experts too
  3. Design content to solve problems and stay out of the way

The idea that hands-on learning is more effective led us to fork the Swagger project, whose features leverage the way HMDA API was designed to create an interactive console. However, to avoid creating an insular experience, I pushed for a pedagogic vision–one that introduces concepts approachably and reinforces them before handing over the keys to the car.

Prototype

Initial concept validation was done using paper prototypes and wireframes. To develop and explore it yet remain lean, we turned to Github. Forking the Eregs documentation repository allowed us to prototype our static content and visual explorations. Forking Swagger's repo allowed us to prototype our interactive content.

Internal validation

External testing

We performed usability testing at various stages of development with expert and non-expert audiences to figure out our pain points and to plan future iterations. Because we also had specific goals for UI elements, particularly around using plain language while preserving familiarity, our usability testing went beyond "general impression"-types of tasks. Github played a major role in relaying feedback, using Issues to request, discuss, and track changes. It allowed the entire team to join the conversation and follow UX's process–especially when we had to do it again the next year. With a new team. In two months.

Final thoughts

I learned a lot from this project. It's too easy to forget that developers are also people, also users. While I have experience in front end developent, I essentially had to teach myself how an API works, how to interact with it in order for it to bear fruit. It was important for me to empathize as much as possible with the developer if I was truly to fill a user experience role and not one of a front end coder or copywriter. This led to my belief that a pedagogical approach to software documentation is something that will benefit the developer community as is but also make it more welcoming for others to be a part of it. I also learned a lot about how users felt about the CFPB's open data/transparent government. With this knowledge I know how to empathize with my current HMDA team–a team of full-time x developers and one designer-as we prepare to publish 2013 data, and (a synonym therefore/then) with the broad HMDA audience.
