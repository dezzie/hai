API Docs

Home Mortgage Disclosure Act (Hum-Dah)

Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) ·

Engaging the developer community with engaging, efficient software documentation.

Read a 7-minute case study

The CFPB believes that making public data easy to use is necessary for government transparency. It's a major part of why they release open source software and engage the developer community. With the launch of HMDA, the Bureau saw an opportunity to showcase its toughest API, but they wanted to get their documentation right. Serving in both user experience and front-end roles, I used lean UX methods to design efficient content, and reached out to the local developer community for usability testing.

Context

During the discovery phase of the HMDA project, we heard from its technologist stakeholders that they'd like to show the developer community how the CFPB uses technology to improve the financial lives of consumers. The HMDA API, they explained, was the perfect opportunity to leverage a federally-mandated public data project and get civic-minded techies involved. The HMDA team had used its own API to build a useful tool; unleashed to the public, the sky was the limit.

Unlike the greater HMDA project, where delivering data about the mortgage industry was the end goal, here HMDA data was the means to a different end; a strong call to action for nerds everywhere to serve their country from behind the soft glow of their computer screens.

Concept

So we needed to address them directly, not as adjuncts to advocacy groups or mortgage data experts (the primary audiences of HMDA). First and foremost, we needed to provide the means for success. As a designer, I wanted to create documentation that solved problems efficiently, but I also didn't want to shut out anyone with no previous experience. They could have great ideas too.

Working closely with the API's author, Clinton Dreisbach, and visual designer Mehgan Iulo, our concept was designed to meet these goals:

  1. Bring content in line with CFPB brand and voice–this included a heuristic analysis of extant documentation;
  2. Edit Clinton's personal documentation into plain language;
  3. Design content into interactions that solved problems and stayed out of the way.

Prototyping

Initial concept validation was done using paper prototypes. Forking the Eregs documentation repository allowed us to validate our ideas about reinforcing concepts, titrate the level of technical detail vs plain language, and visually explore the brand.

The idea that experiential learning is more effective led us to fork the Swagger project, which would leverage the way HMDA API was designed to generate interactive documentation.

We learned from the prototype phase of the project that the main navigation served as linear way for newcomers to explore the API. The Swagger console served as the preferred way to look up fields and definitions. We customized Swagger's code to best answer that need.

In addition to writing and editing the content in the prototype, all front-end work (responsive, too) was completed by me.

Internal validation

This the staff directory photo of Bill Shelton, the software development lead at the CFPB. Sometime near the end of the business day on January 5, 2014 he remarked,

"This is the best API documentation I have ever seen."

You'll have to take my word for it. After all, lean UX doesn't like to be in the deliverables business.

On a less facetious vein, we also used Github in this project for internal validation. Pull requests allow team members to review work submissions before signing off on them. The pull request history for this project is public.

External testing

In April of 2013, I teamed up with UX'ers interested in government API usability at 18f to learn how to better engage our common audience. In particular, 18f wanted to find what made HMDA API's documentation successful. I gave the 18f group a short talk about the goals I wanted to validate before having 3 private sector developers walk me through their interactions with the content. All deliverables were open-sourced on Github. They, and many of my one-liners, were used to promote usability testing on other government projects.

Face-time and actionable feedback strengthens the relationship between government and citizen developers, ultimately resulting in better service to consumers. If sparked a conversation too. After the session, one of our testers blogged about why he appreciated it and thought it was important.

Learning from user behavior

The most valuable thing learned from usability testing wasn't whether developers found the CFPB's documentation authoritative yet approachable (which they did), or if it was easy to understand and efficient (that too). Rather, it was insight about Bureau efforts to engage and recruit from their audience. These were the biggest take-aways:
1. Developers appreciated being able to use the API immediately.

This spoke to the concept of reinforcement, where small digestible pieces of information were followed with ways to reinforce them, or move onto a more complex version. In the content, all queries and examples were linked up to the interactive console, where users could reinforce or test their learning with their own examples.
2. Interactive documentation is expected.

The PDF's, Word docs (yep), and wiki pages found in other areas of government documentation makes devs suspicious about the sophistication of an API, and gives the impression of stale, lazy content.
3. Govspeak is a turn-off.

When acronyms, insider terms, and obscure abbreviations are here to stay, using plain language everywhere else possible might make the difference. Might.
4. Commitment.

Those devs who are willing to invest their business or time into using government APIs need reassurance that the Bureau will maintain it responsibly. The CFPB is ultimately responsible for collecting, releasing, and verifying much of its data.

"It was actually candid. Prior to the session, I was asked to be candid with my feedback. Sure. Everyone asks for candid feedback. But when you really get to the nitty gritty of telling someone their baby looks like a toaster oven, the results can be less than productive. However, this session actually was candid. The environment allowed me to be as honest as necessary and I got the sense the openness was actually appreciated."
5. Devs are users too.

Putting as much time and effort into getting to know the developer or open source audience makes them feel heard. As a result, they will want to get to know the Bureau.

Iteration

In order to prove to the developer community that their feedback was not given in vain, I synthesized it into Github Issues. A feature used by devs and designers alike to discuss solutions and manage tasks, I leveraged them to document for future iterations on this project. It also allows any creative in the public sphere to contribute to the Bureau's open source mission in this way.

This backlog of fixes and challenges is public. Each includes the who, what, when, where, why and how of the current state (bulbasaur) and desired future state (ivysaur). I was able to give a more in-depth talk on the process of using Github to carry out this aspect of user experience work. Those mildly saucy and opinionated slides are here.
