How it all began

We learned from the first round of user testing that the visual aids we created to help testers compare fees were being ignored. Clean, bold, larger type was just as effective at getting their attention. Whether or not a fee was relatively higher or lower than an industry standard was not the persuasive factor in tester's choices.

User testing goals

The business wanted to know if consumers would be likely to comparison shop between prepaid cards if they had a consistent fee disclosure on every card's package.

Competitive Analysis

All in all, you see a hybrid of the Schumer box and nutrition facts label going on.

Initial constraints

You can see how Pew's concept starts falling apart when you plug in all the information that would be required by law. Pew advocated for disclosing any and all information to consumers pre-purchase. From a packaging perspective, this would be very tricky to implement for material reasons alone.

Form structure

From Design's initial work, what was working was a strong type hierarchy. Four prominent fees were driving users' decisions in testing. The business had chosen those fees arbitrarily. I saw an opportunity to elicit a reaction from consumers–those fees needed to mean something more than an amount of money.

Designing the behavioral plan

I used Steve Wendel's Designing for Behavior Change Toolkit to visualize the behaviors we wanted and challenges we would face.

You can see that we focused on giving strong emphasis to four fees as a way to grab consumers' attention. Our biggest challenge was working with the business to minimize the amount (and location) of fine print.

Our form is meant to be used on the most common type of card packaging. It is placed where the consumer is used to finding more information about a card product.

The business wanted to know how likely consumers were to look for additional information in a card's package. Some advocacy groups we talked to felt that the only fair disclosure was a full one. Our prototype experiments with a fold-out disclosure in a resealable part of a package.

Design proposed a digital version of a full disclosure instead. Using a call to action on the disclosure form, consumers could use a short URL to quickly pull up the terms and conditions for a card product. This would help solve any problems of package and material durability. Consumers are generally weary of the "you break you buy" principle.

I also set up a prototype SMS fee-lookup service. Using platforms like Textizen, the 90% of Americans with text-messaging access could look up fees this way.
